DVFS (Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling) controller.
This is the core power management hardware in GPUs and SoCs that adjusts voltage
and frequency based on workload demand, temperature, and power budget.

Inputs:
- clk: system clock
- rst: active-high async reset
- workload[7:0]: current workload intensity (0=idle, 255=max load)
- temperature[7:0]: current die temperature reading (0=cold, 255=hot)
- temp_threshold[7:0]: temperature above which thermal throttling activates
- power_budget[7:0]: maximum allowed power consumption

Outputs:
- freq_sel[2:0]: frequency level (0=lowest, 7=highest)
- voltage_sel[2:0]: voltage level (0=lowest, 7=highest)
- throttled: 1 when thermal throttling is active
- power_est[7:0]: estimated power consumption (level*level, simplified V*f model)

Behavior:
- Workload maps to target DVFS level: 0-31→level 0, 32-63→level 1, ..., 224-255→level 7
- freq_sel and voltage_sel always equal current_level (coupled scaling)
- Ramps up/down one level per clock cycle (safe voltage transitions, no jumping)
- Thermal throttling: when temperature >= temp_threshold, throttled=1 and level ramps down to max level 2
- Power budget: will not ramp up if (next_level)^2 > power_budget
- power_est = current_level * current_level (simplified power model)
- Reset sets everything to 0

Verify:
1. Reset initializes all outputs to 0
2. Workload-to-level mapping is correct for each range
3. Ramps up one level per cycle (not instant jump)
4. Ramps down one level per cycle
5. Thermal throttling activates when temperature >= threshold
6. Thermal throttling clamps level to max 2
7. Power budget prevents ramp-up when (level+1)^2 > budget
8. freq_sel and voltage_sel always match
9. power_est = level * level
10. Thermal throttle overrides workload demand
